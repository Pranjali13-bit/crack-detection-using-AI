CRACK TYPES

Each crack pattern is trained with 200+ reference images for accurate classification.

Low Risk
Hairline Cracks
Fine surface cracks <0.2mm width. Common in concrete curing. Usually cosmetic but monitored for progression.
Critical
Structural Cracks
Deep cracks through the full depth of a member. Indicate overloading or foundation failure. Requires urgent attention.
Moderate
Diagonal / Shear Cracks
45° angle cracks in beams/columns. Indicate shear stress exceeding capacity. Common at supports.
Severe
Horizontal Cracks
Run parallel to ground in walls. Indicate lateral soil/water pressure or sliding failure in retaining walls.
Low Risk
Vertical Cracks
Vertical fractures in walls or columns. Often from differential settlement, shrinkage, or thermal expansion.
Severe
Corrosion Cracks
Rust staining + spalling around rebar. Rebar corrosion expands and splits concrete cover. Needs immediate repair.
Moderate
Map / Crazing Cracks
Network of fine cracks across a surface (spider-web pattern). Caused by alkali-silica reaction or plastic shrinkage.
Severe
Flexural Cracks
Perpendicular cracks at beam midspan tension zone. Indicate bending stress exceeding tensile capacity of concrete.

BEAM
Horizontal Load-Bearing Member

Beams carry bending moments and shear forces. Crack patterns directly indicate the type of structural stress. Flexural cracks at midspan suggest bending overload; diagonal cracks near supports indicate shear failure.

Flexural Shear/Diagonal Torsional Corrosion Hairline
COLUMN
Vertical Compression Member

Columns carry axial loads plus bending moments from lateral forces. Vertical splitting cracks indicate pure compression failure; diagonal cracks suggest combined bending and shear. Column damage is critical — requires immediate assessment.

Vertical Splitting Diagonal Corrosion Spalling
SLAB
Flat Horizontal Floor/Roof Plate

Slabs distribute loads in two directions. Punching shear cracks appear around columns; flexural cracks appear on the tension face. Shrinkage cracks are common on the top surface. Map cracking indicates AAR or carbonation.

Punching Shear Flexural Map/Crazing Shrinkage
WALL
Vertical Structural / Shear Wall

Walls resist both gravity and lateral loads. Diagonal cracks in X-pattern (X-cracks) are classic shear wall failure signatures. Horizontal cracks indicate sliding. Step cracks in masonry follow mortar joints.

Diagonal/X-Crack Horizontal Vertical Step Crack
FOUNDATION
Footing and Base Element

Foundation cracks are among the most critical. Settlement cracks propagate upward through the structure. Heave cracks point inward. Corrosion in reinforcement causes progressive section loss. Requires geotechnical investigation.

Settlement Heave Corrosion Flexural
BRIDGE
Deck, Pier and Girder

Bridge components experience fatigue loading from traffic. Delamination and spalling on decks, flexural cracks in girders, and corrosion at expansion joints are primary concerns. Critical infrastructure requiring regular inspection.

Fatigue Delamination Flexural Corrosion
RETAINING WALL
Earth-Retaining Structure

Retaining walls face continuous lateral earth and water pressure. Horizontal cracks indicate sliding failure; overturning causes tension cracks on the back face. Drainage failure accelerates deterioration.

Horizontal Overturning Settlement Seepage
STAIRCASE
Stair Slab, Waist and Landing

Stair slabs are thin inclined plates. Cracks typically appear at the soffit (underside) along the waist. Nosing cracks at tread edges are common from concentrated loads. Landing-wall junctions are critical zone for shrinkage cracks.

Waist Cracks Nosing Cracks Shrinkage Flexural
